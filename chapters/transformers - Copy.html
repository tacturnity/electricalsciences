1. Introduction and Basic Model (Page 929)

A transformer fundamentally consists of two coils (inductors) that are magnetically coupled. When a primary and secondary terminal are connected as shown in Figure 14.20a, this setup can be modeled by an equivalent circuit made of three uncoupled inductors, as shown in Figure 14.20b (the "T-equivalent" model).

  • Figure 14.20a: Shows the basic representation of a transformer with two coupled coils, L1 and L2, sharing a mutual inductance M.
  • Figure 14.20b: Presents an equivalent circuit using three separate inductors (L1−M, L2−M, and M) which behaves the same way regarding terminals v1, i1 and v2, i2.

2. Core Losses (Pages 929-930)

Transformers, especially those with magnetic cores like iron, experience power losses within the core material. There are two main types:

  • Eddy Currents: Because the core material (like iron) is conductive, the time-varying magnetic flux inside it induces circulating currents within the core itself (depicted in Figure 14.21a). These currents cause resistive heating (RI2) loss. The power loss due to eddy currents (Pe) is given by:
    Pe = Ke f2 Bm2
    Where Bm is the maximum flux density, f is the frequency, and Ke is a constant.
  • Hysteresis Loss (Ph): Related to the energy lost as the magnetic domains in the core reorient themselves with the changing magnetic field. (Detailed discussion referenced on page 929).

Reducing Eddy Current Loss: To minimize these losses, transformer cores are often constructed from thin sheets, called laminations (Figure 14.21b), insulated from each other (e.g., by varnish). This increases the resistance path for eddy currents, significantly lowering Pe loss (page 930).

Total Core Loss: The sum of hysteresis loss (Ph) and eddy-current loss (Pe) is the total core loss (P). An expression is (page 930):

P = Ph + Pe = Kh f Bm + Ke f2 Bm2
(Note: The PDF uses Bm for Ph, often an approximation. A general form uses Bmn, where n is the Steinmetz exponent. The copied LaTeX follows the text's formula.)

3. The Ideal Transformer (Pages 930-934)

To simplify analysis, especially for iron-core transformers in power systems, the concept of an "ideal transformer" is used.

Coupling Coefficient (k): Quantifies the degree of magnetic coupling between primary (L1) and secondary (L2) coils sharing mutual inductance M. Defined from non-negative energy storage (page 931): M ≤ √(L1 L2).

k = M √(L1 L2)
  • 0 ≤ k ≤ 1
  • k ≈ 0: Loosely coupled (e.g., air-core)
  • k ≈ 1: Tightly coupled (e.g., iron-core)

Perfect Coupling (k=1): Implies (Figure 14.22, page 932):

M = √(L1 L2)

Turns Ratio (N): Since inductance L ∝ N2 / ℛ, the turns ratio N is defined as (page 932):

N = √(L2/L1) = N2 N1

Where N1 and N2 are the number of turns on the primary and secondary coils.

Ideal Transformer Definition: A transformer is ideal if (page 932):

  • Perfect coupling (k=1).
  • Infinite self-inductances (L1, L2 → ∞) such that their ratio (N) remains constant.

Ideal Transformer Relationships: For an ideal transformer (schematic Figure 14.23, page 933), voltages (V1, V2) and currents (I1, I2) are related by the turns ratio N:

V2 = N V1
I2 = − I1 N
(Note: The negative sign (−) in current arises from the dot convention for power conservation).

An ideal transformer is a lossless device (absorbs zero instantaneous power, p = v1 i1 + v2 i2 = 0, page 933) and stores zero average energy (page 934). Figures 14.24 and 14.25 (page 933) show equivalent circuit models using controlled sources.

4. Impedance Matching (Pages 935-937)

Ideal transformers are used for impedance matching, changing how a load impedance appears to the source. Consider Figure 14.27 (page 936): a source Vg with internal impedance Zg connected via an ideal transformer to a load ZL.

  • Reflected Impedance: The load impedance ZL connected to the secondary appears to the primary side as ZL / N2.
  • Input Impedance: The total impedance seen by the source Vg is (page 936):
Vg I1 = Zg + ZL N2    (Impedance seen by source: Zin = Zg + ZL/N2)

Application: By choosing N appropriately, the reflected impedance ZL / N2 can be matched to the source impedance Zg (or its complex conjugate) for maximum power transfer (Example 14.11 context, page 937-938).

5. Nonideal-Transformer Models (Pages 938-941)

For accuracy, practical (nonideal) aspects are included. Figure 14.31 (page 939) shows a common model for a practical iron-core transformer (referred to the primary side).

Model Components:

  • Rs: Equivalent series resistance (copper losses in windings).
  • Xs: Equivalent series leakage reactance (flux linking only one coil).
  • Rm: Core loss resistance (shunt branch - hysteresis & eddy currents).
  • Xm: Magnetizing reactance (shunt branch - establishing core flux).

Currents:

  • I1: Primary current.
  • I2' = −NI2: Secondary current referred to the primary.
  • Ie: Exciting current (through Rm and Xm). No-load primary current.
  • Im: Magnetizing current (component of Ie through Xm).

Parameter Determination: Model parameters are found using tests:

  • Open-Circuit Test: Rated voltage on one side, other open (I2=0). Measures input V, I (Ie), P. Primarily determines shunt parameters Rm and Xm (Figure 14.32, Pages 939-940).
  • Short-Circuit Test: Reduced voltage on primary for rated current in shorted secondary. Measures input V, I (I1), P. Shunt current Ie is negligible. Primarily determines series parameters Rs and Xs (Pages 940-941).

6. Transformer Properties (Page 942)

Two important figures of merit:

  • Efficiency (Eff): Ratio of output power (Po) to input power (Pi). Pi = Po + Ploss, where Ploss = Pcopper + Pcore.
    Eff = Po Pi
    Eff = Po Po + Pl    (using Pl for loss)
    Eff = Pi − Pl Pi = 1 − Pl Pi
  • Voltage Regulation (VR): Measures output voltage change from no load (VNL) to full load (VFL), as % of VFL.
    VR = VNL − VFL VFL × 100%
    Lower VR percentage is generally better (more stable output voltage).

7. Frequency Considerations (Pages 944-945)

While power transformers operate at fixed frequency (e.g., 60 Hz), others (e.g., audio) operate over a range (e.g., 30 Hz - 15,000 Hz).

  • Audio Transformer Model: Includes winding capacitances (C1, C2 in Figure 14.34), significant at high frequencies.
  • Frequency Response (Figure 14.35):
    • Low Frequencies: Small magnetizing impedance Xm = 2πfLm shunts current, reducing output V2.
    • Mid-Frequencies (e.g., 100 Hz - 10 kHz): Output relatively constant.
    • High Frequencies: Leakage reactance Xs increases, shunt capacitance impedance decreases, reducing output. Resonance between L and C can occur.
  • Good Audio Transformer: Needs large magnetizing inductance Lm (high permeability core) for good low-frequency performance.
